What is an instanton?

The technical answer:


Instantons are classical finite action solutions of the euclidean equations of motion. They are chracterized by a non-vanishing topological charge.

  • Why classical?
    • Classical solutions are of interest because they can serve as a
    startting point for the semi-classical expansion in field theory.
    This is most easily seen from the Feynman path integral: In the
    limit h->0, the dominant path corresponds to a solution of the
    classical equations of motion. Quantum corrections correspond
    to fluctuations around the classical path.
    In ordinary perturbation theory, we expand vacuum amplitudes
    around the trivial classical vacuum. But many important non-
    perturbative phenomena (e.g. tunneling) can not be studied this
    way (they do not appear at any finite order in this expansion).
    Instead, we have to find more appropriate, non-trivial, vacuum
    configurations to expand around.
  • Why euclidean?
    • There is a naive, a physical, and a practical answer to that question.
    The naive one: Many interesting field theories have no stable, localized
    (soliton) solutions in Minkowski space.
    The more physical one: Classical solutions in euclidean space have
    special significance because they correspond to tunneling events in
    Minkowski space. This is an example for the kind of situation mentioned
    above, a physical effect that cannot be discussed in terms of ordinary
    perturbation theory.
    And the practical one: Non-perturbative approaches to quantum field
    theory (e.g. the lattice) are best formulated in euclidean space
    (where everything is positive and looks like statistical mechanics).
    To understand what happens in lattice field theory, we need configurations
    that give important contributions to the euclidean partition function.
  • Why topology?
    • The stability of the instanton solution is related to the existence
    of a conserved charge. But this charge is not associated with a continous symmetry of the theory (the instanton has vacuum quantum numbers), but
    with the topological properties of the instanton. The instanton has a non-zero ``winding number", and no continous transformation of the
    fields can unwind the instanton solution.
    The topological properties of the instanton have many important
    consequences. For example, fields with non-trivial topology are
    intimately connected with anomalies, classical symmetries of the
    theory that are violated on the quantum level.

The practical answer:

Instantons describe tunneling events between degenerate classical vacua.
